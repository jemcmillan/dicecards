Create Experiences

3 Levels of an Experience

  1. Touchpoints. They are actions initiated by a physical or sensory interaction. Each touchpoint is a moment that connects to a user in a way to create an individual experience. Examples of touchpoints include:
    • Touching an information screen.
    • The interactation with a display item.
    • Experiencing a specific sound or visual effect designed to elicit a reaction.
    • The moment someone views a poster, infographic or piece of art.
  2. Engagements. Engagments are An experience typically includes multiple engagements but may sometimes be focused on a single engagement. Examples of engagements include:
    • Displays, booths, and demonstrations.
    • Presentations, briefings, and speeches.
    • Activity, games, and rides.
    • Digital posts on websites and social media.
  3. Events. Events are a collection of organzied engagements. An event usually has an overarching concept or theme. Examples of an event include: a Fleet Week, an art show, an auto race, a ship tour, or a festival. An event can hold smaller events. For example, a Fleet Week event can hold a ship tour event, an air show event, and a festival.

Relationships between levels

- Events can be made up of smaller events.

- Events typically have multiple engagements.

- Engagements have at least one touchpoint.

- Touchpoints are the moments of interaction between the person and the engagement.

Importance

The importance of understanding the three levels is that we want to give each level consideration when designing the experience. We can use the 5E Model (Excitement, Entry, Engagement, Exit, Extension) and the DBITs framework (Duration, Intensity, Breadth, Interaction, Triggers, Significance) at each level of an experience. Using the 5E Model and DBITS framework will help you design an experience that reaches people at the visceral, behavioral and reflective level.

  • Visceral

    The visceral level is the most basic level of processing. It is how we make quick judgments about a situation, e.g., good or bad, safe or dangerous. It responds to the immediate present in which the emotions that come up are unaffected by context or history. No cause, blame, or credit is assigned to the reaction.

    Don Norman writes in 'The Design of Everyday Things':

    "For designers, the visceral response is about immediate perception: the pleasantness of a mellow, harmonious sound or the jarring, irritating scratch of fingernails on a rough surface. Here is where the style matters: appearances, whether sound or sight, touch or smell, drive the visceral response. This has nothing to do with how usable, effective, or understandable the product is. It is all about attraction or repulsion. Great designers use their aesthetic sensibilities to drive these visceral responses."

  • Behavioral

    The behavioral level of processing is the home of learned skills. Actions, reactions and analysis at this level are mostly subconscious. If people have a good understanding and knowledge of the expected outcome, the behavioral states will make them feel in control. However, people may feel frustration and anger when things don’t go as planned, especially when neither the reason nor the possible remedies are known.

    Don Norman writes:

    "For designers, the most critical aspect of the behavioral level is that every action is associated with an expectation. Expect a positive outcome and the result is a positive affective response (a positive valence, in the scientific literature). Expect a negative outcome and the result is a negative affective response (a negative valence): dread and hope, anxiety and anticipation."

  • Reflective

    The reflective level of processing where conscious cognition happens. This is where reasoning, conscious decision-making and understanding develop. Visceral and behavioral levels are subconscious, so they respond rapidly. Reflection is cognitive, more analytical and slower, often occurring long after an event has occurred or a product has been viewed.

    Don Norman again:

    "To the designer, reflection is perhaps the most important of the levels of processing. Reflection is conscious, and the emotions produced at this level are the most protracted: those that assign agency and cause, such as guilt and blame or praise and pride. Reflective memories are often more important than reality. If we have a strongly positive visceral response but disappointing usability problems at the behavioral level, when we reflect back upon the product, the reflective level might very well weigh the positive response strongly enough to overlook the severe behavioral difficulties."
