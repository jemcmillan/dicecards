The PROOF Principle

Regardless of the size or scope of the design itself, evaluations should adhere to the PROOF principles: Evaluations should be pragmatic, realistic, objective, open and fully integrated.

  • Pragmatic. Evaluations should fit the scope of the project. A simple project with limited objectives and audiences may not require the same level of evaluation as a complex communication plan. Everything should be evaluated to some extent, but you get to choose the level of evaluation.
  • Realistic. Be realistic about what information you can evaluate and the validity of that information. You may not have the time or resources to conduct random, valid surveys to determine the effects of a campaign but may be able to pulse audience segments and collect insights. Design a realistic plan that will help you understand and explain the effects of the campaign.
  • Objective. Understand what the evaluation means. Try to understand any biases that may be occur for the evaluation method used. Be aware of limitations of the evaluation and take those limitations into account when analyzing and reporting results.
  • Open. Be open to what the evaluation tells you. Don't discount evalations that don't line up with your viewpoint. Be sure to document and share any evaluation method biases and limitations in your report. The more open you are about the limitations of the evaluation, the more trustworthy the overall evaluation is.
  • Fully-integrated. Start thinking about evalutions early in the design process and ensure they are fully-integrated throughout the creation and delivery stages. Ideally, you built evaluations into the plan after the Ideation phase of the DICE model.
