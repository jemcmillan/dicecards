Develop the Plan

Communication plans can be simple, complex or anywhere in between. No matter what level of complexity they take, all communication plans should contain the following:

Problem Statement

Include the problem statement or set of problem statements. The problem statement was written during the DEFINE stage of the DICE model. This is the overall problem the organization is trying to solve.

Problem Statement Example: Over the past six months, six people in our organization were arrested for driving under the influence. Over the past three years, an average of two people per year were arrested for DUI. How can we help our people not drive while under the influence of alcohol or drugs?

Objectives

Objectives are defined activities that could help solve the problem. The best objectives are written as SMART Objectives:

  • Specific. What is to be done? "How will you know it is done?" and describes the end product of the work to be done. The description is written in such a way that anyone reading the objective will most likely interpret it the same way.
  • Measurable. How will you know it meets expectations? Enand defines the objective using assessable terms like quantity, quality, frequency, costs, deadlines, etc. It refers to the extent to which something can be evaluated against some standard.
  • Achievable. Can the objective be accomplished? Does the team have the experience, knowledge or capability to fulfill the expectation? Can it be done giving the time frame, opportunity and resources?
  • Relevant. To ensure the objective is relevant ask: 1) Should it be done? 2) Why should it be done? 3) What will be the impact? and 4) Is the objective aligned with the organization's strategic plan?
  • Time-oriented. When does the objective need to be done? An objective needs to have end points and check points built into it. Sometimes a task may only have an end point or due date.

Objective Example: Decrease the number of DUIs from our organization by 50% over the next six months.

Sub-Objectives

Sub-objectives are the individual activities, efforts, and channels that will be used to meet the objective. Sub-objectives include the following:

  • Defined audiences. Specifically define your target audiences. Who's support is needed to achieve the objective? Who are you trying to inform, influence or motivate?
  • Identified tools. What tools will you use to achieve the objective? What content will need to be created? What experiences need to be designed?
    • Established timetable. Once all these elements have been considered, and preferably documented, develop an overall timeline that can be followed.
  • Evaluation framework. What will you be able to measure to determine success?

Sub-Objective Examples:

Sub-Objective 1: Increase the number of team members who know how to use the organization's “Safe Ride” program to 75% over the next six months.

Sub-Objective 2: Decrease team members’ reported reluctance to use “Safe Ride” program, taxi cabs or ride-share programs to 25% over the next six months.

Sub-Objective 3: Increase willingness of peers to intervene to stop team members (known and unknown) from driving while under the influence of alcohol to 90%.
