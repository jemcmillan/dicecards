Mindset: Divergent Thinking

Brainstorm Solutions

Brainstorming is a way to generate a lot of ideas by leveraging the creative power of a group. It's accomplished by engaging with each other, listening, and building on other ideas. Brainstorming is the most frequently practiced form of ideation.

Brainstorm for:

  • Solutions to the problem statements.
  • For content solutions that can be applied to the Communication Box Model.
  • For a high concept experience.

Nine Thoughts on Brainstorming

  1. Set a time limit. Within the time limit, the sole goal to come up with as many ideas as possible. All ideas are welcome and all judgements of those ideas are prohibited. Brainstorming sessions usally last around 15-60 minutes.
  2. Stay focued on the problem statement Alex Osborn, the father of the Brainstorming technique, emphasises that brainstorming sessions should always address a specific question or problem statement as sessions addressing multiple questions are inefficient. Begin with a good problem statement or question.
  3. Defer judgement or criticism This includes non-verbals. Participants should reserve criticism for a later stage in the ideation process. A Brainstorming session is not the time and the place to evaluate ideas, and you should avoid executional details.
  4. Encourage wild ideas. Any idea should be documented. The more wild, the better. The more unique, the better. Wild ideas have a way of turning up the creativity of the brainstorm participants. This creates more wild ideas and helps teams find creative solutions.
  5. Aim for quantity. Aim for as many new ideas as possible. The assumption is that the greater the number of ideas you generate, the bigger your chance is of producing a radical and effective solution. Brainstorming celebrates the maxim “quantity breeds quality."
  6. Build on each others' ideas. Brainstorming stimulates the building of ideas by a process of association. Embrace the most out-of-the-box notions and build, build, build. Be positive and build on the ideas of others. Brainstorming works well when participants use each other's ideas to trigger their own thinking. Our minds are highly associative. One thought easily triggers another. When we use the thoughts of others, then these will stop us getting trapped by our own thinking structures.
  7. Move up and down the ladder of abstraction. The Ladder of Abstraction is a continuum from the concrete (base of the ladder) to the abstract (top of the ladder). Take ideas up and down -- make them more or less abstract -- and see what new ideas the team comes up with.
  8. Be visual. Sketch your idea. Put all the ideas on post-it notes, on a dry-erase board, or on large flip charts.
  9. One conversation at a time. Listen to each other and elaborate on each other’s ideas. Don’t get obsessed with your own ideas. You’re here to ideate together.
